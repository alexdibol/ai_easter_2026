Course Orientation

The course frames AI as an institutional production system for financial judgment. It connects infrastructure, model mechanics, prompt engineering, agents, governance maturity, and frontier AI to the practical responsibilities of modern finance.

Purpose: Build AI literacy for financial professionals without reducing AI to hype, demos, or casual automation.
Method: Combine books, decks, podcasts, videos, notebooks, and handbooks into a chapter-by-chapter learning system.
Governance: Teach evidence boundaries, verification, auditability, reproducibility, escalation, and human accountability.
Course Framework

Capability and control must rise together

The course is not a generic introduction to artificial intelligence. It is a professional formation in AI as architecture, institutional capability, and governed practice. Finance students and professionals learn how AI systems are built, how they behave, how they fail, how they can be supervised, and how they can be responsibly deployed in high-accountability financial environments.

1

Technical Literacy

Students learn the mechanics of infrastructure, data, representation, training, inference, model families, prompt systems, agents, and modern AI architectures without needing to become machine learning researchers.

2

Financial Application

The course connects AI methods to finance: risk, credit, valuation, disclosure, due diligence, portfolio construction, document interpretation, scenario generation, and decision support.

3

Governance Discipline

Governance is treated as a core design principle: evidence boundaries, structured outputs, verification status, deterministic gates, audit bundles, reproducibility, and human review are part of the system.

Course Architecture

Six volumes plus a final capstone

The curriculum is organized as a six-volume ladder. The sequence moves from infrastructure to model mechanics, from controlled generation to agents, from governance maturity to modern AI, and finally toward a synthetic capstone operating model.

Volume 01

AI Infrastructure

AI as an industrial and institutional production system shaped by compute, cloud, deployment economics, and governance.

Volume 02

AI and ML 101

Mechanical literacy in model families, representation, training, inference, diagnostics, evaluation, and failure modes.

Volume 03

Prompt Engineering

Prompting as a governance discipline built around contracts, evidence boundaries, schemas, and fail-closed generation.

Volume 04

AI Agents

Agents as bounded workflow components inside auditable orchestration graphs, not as autonomous digital employees.

Volume 05

Governance Maturity

The maturity ladder from experimental use to reusable assets and enterprise-grade governed AI operating models.

Volume 06

Modern AI

Reasoning models, long context, memory, fine-tuning, surrogates, multimodality, Bayesian AI, diffusion, and frontier topics.

Resource Ecosystem

Chapter-by-chapter learning routes

Each substantive chapter supports multiple ways of learning. A student may begin with the deck, watch the video, listen to the podcast, read the chapter, and then open the notebook. Another may begin with implementation, discover conceptual gaps, and return to the book. This recursive movement is intentional: the course is designed not only to be completed, but to be revisited.

A

Books and Papers

Written materials develop the conceptual argument, technical explanation, financial relevance, and governance framing of each topic.

B

Video and Podcast

Videos provide guided explanation, while podcasts offer conversational reinforcement and flexible review before or after class.

C

Specialized Decks and Colab

Specialized decks compress the structure of each chapter, while Colab notebooks make technical ideas executable, inspectable, reproducible, and testable.
